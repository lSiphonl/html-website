Why I Enjoy Coding Complex Subjects

One of the ways I enjoy learning the most is by translating theory into code. Instead of only studying concepts in a passive way, I prefer to implement them and see how they behave in a structured system. For me, coding becomes a tool for understanding, because it forces you to break down ideas into their most fundamental parts.

A good example of this is when I studied Decision Support Systems (CMPG312). Rather than only memorising formulas or procedures, I implemented them in code. This included things like linear programming, simplex tableau methods, assignment problems, and transshipment models. Through coding them, I was able to understand not just how they work, but why they work the way they do.

What Do I Like About It?

The main reasons are

  • It forces me to break abstract concepts into logical steps
  • It reveals gaps in my understanding very quickly
  • It turns theory into something interactive and testable
  • It helps me understand systems on a deeper level rather than surface memorisation
  • It combines my interest in problem solving and software engineering
  • It makes learning more practical instead of purely theoretical
  • It gives me a sense of building something real from academic content
  • It strengthens both my programming and analytical thinking at the same time

One of the most satisfying parts of this process is seeing a complex mathematical or theoretical concept turn into something that actually runs. For example, a simplex algorithm is not just a set of steps on paper, but a structured process that can be implemented, debugged and improved. When everything finally works correctly, it feels like I have taken something abstract and made it tangible.

I enjoy this approach because it aligns with the way I naturally think. I like understanding systems from the inside out, and coding complex subjects allows me to do exactly that. It turns learning into something active, where I am not just consuming information, but reconstructing it in a way that makes sense to me personally.
